Conformance Levels

Three structural levels. Self-declared by deployers. Read by auditors as one input among others.

Not legal advice. Not certification. Not regulatory substitute. This page is a plain-language explainer of Section 6 of the Decision Provenance Standard. The Standard grades a Charter against three Conformance Levels. A grade is not a certification. The Standard does not certify, does not satisfy, and does not substitute for any regulatory, audit, or attestation regime. Counsel and auditors form their own substantive judgments using a grade as one input among others.

Jurisdiction Assumed: U.S. federal and Delaware as primary; UK, EU AI Act, and Israel as named secondaries. Whether a Member-State implementation of the EU AI Act, an FTC truth-in-advertising review, or a sector regulator's substantive review treats a Level 3 grade as a sufficient input is a question only counsel in that jurisdiction can answer.

The Standard defines three Conformance Levels — 1, 2, and 3 — each with structural criteria a deployer's Charter and decision-record discipline must meet. Levels are self-declared, not certified. Section 6 grades the Charter against the criteria; auditors and counsel form their own substantive judgments using the grading as one input.

The three levels

Level 1

Structural

The deployer's Charter exists, names the decision authority, and identifies the artifact set. Level 1 is the entry threshold: the decision pipeline is named, even if not yet recorded.

Level 2

Recorded

Decision records are produced for every decision the Charter governs. Mode is named; authorship is named; downstream readers can reconstruct what happened from the records alone.

Level 3

Continuously Auditable

Records are produced as decisions happen, not in retrospect. The reporter protocol emits self-declared signals; the deployer's Charter discipline holds across leadership transitions. Charters authored using the Product Org OS can achieve Level 3.

What “grades” means — and what it does not

The Standard's Section 6 grades the Charter at Conformance Level 1, 2, or 3 against the criteria the Section names. A grade is a structural reading of the Charter. It is not a certification, it does not satisfy any regulatory regime, and it does not substitute for substantive judgment by qualified personnel.

A Level 3 grade does not certify. A Level 3 grade does not satisfy. A Level 3 grade does not substitute. The grade is one input among many that counsel, auditors, and regulators use when they form their own substantive judgments.

Worked example

A deployer installs the Standard for a CPO seat. The seat publishes a Charter naming the decision authority for product-launch decisions, the artifact set the seat produces, and the dispatch grammar (Mode 1 / Mode 2). Decision records are produced contemporaneously for every launch decision.

The seat self-declares Conformance Level 2: records exist, mode is named, authorship is named. The seat does not yet emit Reporter API signals continuously, so it does not declare Level 3.

An auditor reading the Charter and records sees a Level 2 grade. The auditor forms a substantive judgment about whether the records support the audit work using the grade as one input. The grade does not certify the seat's posture; it describes the structure the seat operates.

Beyond Level 3

Section 6 of the Standard explicitly excludes Level 4. Beyond Level 3 sits substantive judgment — the qualified personnel work of counsel, auditors, and regulators — which is not the Standard's scope. The Standard formalizes structure; it does not opine on substance.
